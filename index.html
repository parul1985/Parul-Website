About me

I am a PhD candidate at the Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering, Rutgers University. I am a member of the Cyber-Physical Systems Lab directed by Dr. Dario Pompili. My research interests span mobile computing, distributed computing, and wireless networks. My thesis work focuses on harnessing the paradigm of approximate computing to enable applications in resource-limited environments. I enjoy designing and prototyping efficient solutions (in terms of time and battery) for real-world applications using machine learning. I am interested in building real-world applications that leverage my algorithmic knowledge as well as developmental skills.

Projects

Enabling Light-weight Object Detection and Tracking in Mobile Devices

There is a disconnect between the relatively heavy computational requirements of the computer vision solutions, and the limited capacities available on mobile autonomous platforms such as mobile phones or drones on which these algorithms are run. In this work we propose to bridge this gap with a novel Markov Decision Process framework that adapts the parameters of the vision algorithms to the incoming video data rather than fixing them apriori. As a concrete example, we test our framework on a object detection and tracking task, showing significant benefits in terms of energy consumption without considerable loss in accuracy on a combination of publicly available and novel datasets.

- ApproxDroid, sumitted to IROS'17

Accelerating Computationally-intensive Applications via Approximation

The existing domain of mobile computing suffers from limited resources such as battery, CPU, and memory, while at the same time the users expect applications to execute seamlessly with near real-time responses. As a result, achieving high energy efficiency while maintaining a high quality of service is challenging. To bridge this gap, we propose a novel approximate computing solution that reduces the amount of computation that the application has to perform which reduces the time and energy consumed by an application with a nominal loss in accuracy. The effectiveness of the proposed approach is validated through extensive simulation and testbed experiments by comparing approximate versus exact-computation performance.

- Approximate Computing, Elsevier PMC'17
- Exploiting the Untapped Potential of Mobile Distributed Computing via Approximation, PerCom'16

Resource Allocation in Mobile Device Clouds

The heterogeneous sensing, computing, communication, and storage capabilities of local and remote resources are exploited to form a "loosely coupled" mobile Cloud, and a novel resource provisioning engine that dynamically takes decisions on "what" and "where" the tasks should be executed in the mobile Cloud is introduced. Comparison of benefits of collaboration between local and Cloud resources with purely local and centralized approaches are presented through exhaustive computer simulations. A resource-to-task mapper for a distributed framework is implemented on Android devices to offload tasks from a device with limited computational capability to other devices in vicinity in a round-robin fashion.
